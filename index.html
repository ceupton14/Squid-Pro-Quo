The IOG Tariff

Intelligent Octopus Go — and why a flat rate is the worst possible estimate

Intelligent Octopus Go (IOG) is a time-of-use tariff with two rates: a cheap off-peak rate for electricity used between 23:30 and 05:30, and a standard Flexible rate for everything else. It is designed specifically for households with EVs and home batteries that shift heavy consumption to the overnight window. That is exactly this household.

Off-peak (23:30–05:30): 6.667p/kWh
On-peak (all other hours): 24.5–28.09p/kWh depending on the month — the published Flexible tariff rate. The off-peak saving is approximately 20p per kWh.

Across the verified working period (Jul 2024–Sep 2025), 73.3% of all import fell in the overnight window. The gap-period figure, verified by GivEnergy, is 70.3%. Applying a flat daytime rate to a household where 70%+ of consumption is overnight is not a neutral approximation — it is a systematic overcharge of approximately 20p on every one of those off-peak units.

Ofgem's licence conditions require suppliers to bill on the tariff to which a customer is subscribed. Where a fault prevents accurate time-of-use metering, the remedy is not to apply the most expensive available rate — it is to use the best available evidence of actual usage patterns, including third-party inverter data.

The Signal Failure

The Ink-visible Meter — a known shutdown, a foreseeable failure, and a missing repair

The smart metre at 1 Cresswell Lane uses a Toshiba SKU2 SMETS2 device with a cellular communications hub. In the DCC Central/South region, the hub uses O2's network to transmit half-hourly data to Octopus. This rural SN16 postcode has borderline signal — and O2 progressively shut down its 2G/3G infrastructure through 2025.

O2's 3G shutdown was a planned, publicly announced event — not an act of God. Ofgem's Operational Licence Conditions (Standard Licence Condition 25A and the Smart Metering Installation Code of Practice) require energy suppliers to proactively replace or upgrade smart metering assets before WAN services are withdrawn. Octopus took no action at this address despite the known shutdown timeline.

The WAN LED on the metre hub went intermittent from October 2025, indicating loss of signal rather than hardware failure. The MESH LED has been permanently off — there are no mesh-capable neighbours in this rural location, so there is no fallback communications path. The metre did not fail spontaneously; it was abandoned on a network that Octopus knew was closing.

Octopus was notified of the problem in writing on 8 January 2025 — nine months before O2's 3G switch-off and fifteen months before the formal complaint. The agent advised monitoring for 20 days. No follow-up was made and no replacement was arranged.

Meter Configuration Fault

Tentacle-Tangled: the metre was never set up to record time-of-use data correctly

The engineer who visited on 1 April 2026 confirmed not one but two separate faults. The first was the O2 signal problem. The second was more fundamental: the metre had never been configured to maintain separate registers for on-peak and off-peak consumption.

This means the metre could not have billed correctly on Intelligent Octopus Go from the day it was installed — even when it was transmitting data. Correct IOG billing requires the metre to distinguish half-hourly consumption by time-of-use period. Without that configuration, the metre was not fit for purpose on this tariff.

Octopus's response — that their T&Cs permit profile-based (flat-rate) billing when half-hourly data is unavailable — addresses only the signal failure. It does not address the prior configuration fault, nor the question of who approved this metre for IOG billing in this state, nor why two years of apparently correct IOG bills were produced from a metre that lacked the registers to support them.

Profile 1 billing — the industry default applied when half-hourly data is absent — assumes a typical annual consumption of around 2,700 kWh. This household consumes approximately 25,000 kWh per year, including EV charging and a home battery. Profile 1 is not a reasonable or proportionate fallback for an IOG customer with ten times the typical consumption.

Octopus's obligation is to supply on the contracted tariff. Where their own equipment prevents them from doing so, the cost of that failure should not be passed to the customer as a back-bill at the most expensive available rate.

Timestamp Corruption

Why Octopus's own gap-period data cannot be used for time-of-use billing

During the gap period, the metre did transmit some data — but with corrupted timestamps. When the metre briefly re-established a signal after days of silence, it transmitted accumulated stored readings with the reconnection timestamp, not the timestamps of the days on which the consumption actually occurred.

11 days in the gap period show more than 50 kWh recorded — against a normal daily average of roughly 30 kWh. The worst case is 18 January 2026: 99.6 kWh recorded in a single calendar day. This is physically impossible — approximately 3.3 times the household's normal daily consumption, compressed into 24 hours because the metre had been offline for days.

These inflated, timestamp-corrupted readings account for 38.3% of all gap-period Octopus kWh. More critically, the compression shifts consumption from the actual time it occurred — largely overnight, off-peak — into arbitrary daytime slots, artificially inflating the apparent on-peak proportion.

Were Octopus to use their own corrupted gap-period data for time-of-use billing rather than the flat rate, the result would still overcharge: their data shows 63.1% off-peak on corrupted days, against the GivEnergy-verified 69.6%. The GivEnergy inverter records at 5-minute resolution with its own internal clock, entirely independent of the DCC network — its timestamps were unaffected throughout.

Separate Fault

Export underpayment — a fault that predates the signal failure

Separately from the import back-billing dispute, GivEnergy data shows that Octopus consistently recorded less export than the inverter exported — even during months with 100% import data coverage, when the metre was fully functional.

August 2025: GivEnergy recorded 767.2 kWh exported. Octopus recorded 670.7 kWh — a shortfall of 96.5 kWh worth £14.48 at the export rate. This month had 100% import data coverage, so the discrepancy cannot be attributed to the signal problem.

This points to a separate fault in the export metre register or the Smart Export Guarantee data submission process. During the gap period, Octopus recorded only 28% of the export the inverter measured — consistent with the metre being dark for most of this period but worsening a pre-existing under-recording problem.
